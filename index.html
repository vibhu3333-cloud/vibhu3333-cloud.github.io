Research agenda

Making Rules Work

My central research program asks how institutional orders acquire the interpretive competence needed for rules to work. Rather than treating institutions only as constraints, incentives, or equilibria, I study them as fields of meaning: historically evolved structures through which actors learn how to make claims, recognize authority, contest procedure, coordinate disagreement, and restrain power.

The current dissertation arc develops this through civic grammar, credible coordination capacity, and constitutional capacity.

Core concepts

Three linked problems

Micro-foundation

Civic Grammar

The historically evolved structure of meanings, procedures, expectations, and interpretive habits through which people translate grievances into recognized public moves rather than extra-procedural domination.

Institutional ecology

Credible Coordination Capacity

The ability of an institutional ecology to articulate rules, route disputes, produce facts, sanction misconduct, and repair broken expectations in ways that participants can recognize as credible.

Historical stock

Civic-Generative Capacity

The macro-level stock accumulated when civic grammar is repeatedly used, transmitted, adapted, and reproduced across generations, creating durable constitutional and associational capability.

Research areas

Institutional intelligibility and civic grammar

How actors learn the meaning of rules, procedures, claims, authority, and restraint before formal institutions can coordinate them.

Constitutional political economy

How constitutional orders survive democratic pressure, executive concentration, legal ambiguity, and institutional stress.

Law, liberty, and polycentric order

How legal pluralism, common-law reasoning, juries, property, contract, and tort can restore practical self-government.

Capital, money, and time-structured production

How savings, production stages, monetary flows, and dynamic disequilibrium shape capital formation and macroeconomic adjustment.
