Research

Institutions as fields of meaning.

My work develops an interpretive turn inside institutional economics: formal rules, incentives, and equilibria work only when actors possess the civic grammar that makes rules publicly intelligible and procedurally usable.

Dissertation arc

Making Rules Work: Essays on Civic Grammar, Credible Coordination, and Constitutional Capacity in New Institutional Economics

01

Civic Grammar

A theory of how institutional intelligibility emerges: how actors learn to convert raw grievances into petitions, lawsuits, procedural objections, constitutional claims, votes, and other recognized civic moves.

02

Credible Coordination Capacity

A theory of how institutional ecologies make rules operational by creating credible channels for fact production, forum routing, sanctioning, repair, and expectation stabilization.

03

Constitutional Capacity

A theory of how repeated civic action accumulates into durable constitutional capability, allowing societies to withstand shocks, contest power, and reproduce ordered liberty over time.

Institutions as Fields of Meaning: Civic Grammar and the Interpretive Turn in Economics

Develops civic grammar as the missing layer between formal rules and effective institutional coordination. The paper engages New Institutional Economics from within and reinterprets credible commitment through the prior emergence of institutional intelligibility.

Credible Coordination Capacity in Private and Associational Orders

Studies how non-state and associational orders generate credible coordination by routing disputes, producing facts, imposing sanctions, and repairing broken expectations through dense institutional ecologies.

Research notes

Civic work

Bihar, law, and practical liberty

A parallel public-facing strand of my work focuses on Bihar and India: how legal reform, civic education, entrepreneurship, local self-government, and institutional imagination can help ordinary people move from dependency on patronage toward a practical grammar of liberty.

This is not separate from the research program. It is the applied version of the same question: how do people learn to make rules work in actual social life?
