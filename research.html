Research

How rules become usable, credible, and liberty-generating.

My research develops a new institutional economics of rule-working: how societies acquire the interpretive grammar, coordination capacity, and accumulated civic competence required for rules to become more than formal constraints. The central question is not only why rules exist, but how people learn to use them, trust them, repair them, and reproduce them across time.

Dissertation arc

Making Rules Work: Essays on Civic Grammar, Credible Coordination, and Constitutional Capacity in New Institutional Economics

01

Civic Grammar

Civic grammar is the micro-level structure through which actors learn how to participate in institutional life. It converts raw grievances, interests, conflicts, and demands into recognizable public moves: petitions, lawsuits, procedural objections, appeals, votes, constitutional claims, contractual arguments, and civic challenges to power.

02

Credible Coordination Capacity

Credible coordination capacity explains how rules become operational in practice. It studies the institutional ecologies that route disputes, produce facts, stabilize expectations, impose sanctions, repair breakdowns, and allow actors to coordinate without requiring omniscient design or constant state command.

03

Civic-Generative Liberty

Civic-generative liberty names the long-run capacity of a society to generate freedom through repeated civic use. Liberty is not treated only as non-interference or formal right, but as an accumulated social capability: the ability of people to make claims, resist arbitrary power, organize associations, govern locally, and reproduce ordered freedom across generations.

Research architecture

From intelligibility to coordination to liberty

The project moves across three levels: the meaning of rules, the credibility of coordination, and the accumulation of constitutional and civic capacity.

Institutions as Fields of Meaning

This strand argues that institutions are not merely incentive structures or constraint systems. They are fields of meaning that make action intelligible. Formal rules can constrain behavior only after actors know what counts as a claim, a violation, an office, a procedure, a legitimate objection, or an abuse of authority.

Civic grammar

Rules as Credible Coordination Devices

This strand studies how rules acquire operational force through forums, associations, reputational systems, evidentiary practices, sanctions, and repair mechanisms. A rule works not simply because it is written, but because an ecology exists that can interpret, apply, enforce, and restore it when expectations break down.

Coordination capacity

Constitutional Capacity and Civic-Generative Liberty

This strand examines how repeated civic action accumulates into a durable stock of constitutional capability. Societies become freer when people repeatedly learn how to contest power without collapsing into violence, patronage, arbitrary command, or extra-procedural domination.

Working papers

Current manuscripts

These papers develop the larger research program across institutional economics, law and economics, constitutional political economy, private ordering, and emergent social order.

Institutions as Fields of Meaning: Civic Grammar and the Interpretive Turn in Economics

Develops civic grammar as the missing interpretive layer between formal rules and effective institutional coordination. The paper engages New Institutional Economics from within and argues that credible commitment presupposes a prior history of institutional intelligibility.

Working paper

Credible Coordination Capacity in Private and Associational Orders

Studies how non-state and associational orders generate credible coordination through dispute routing, fact production, sanctioning, expectation stabilization, and institutional repair. The paper asks how order emerges when formal law is incomplete, distant, slow, or insufficient by itself.

In progress

Civic-Generative Capacity and Constitutional Durability

Explores how the repeated use, transmission, and adaptation of civic grammar produces a long-run stock of constitutional capacity. The paper links micro-level civic competence to macro-level constitutional durability, especially the ability to resist arbitrary power without dissolving into coercive bypass or patronage.

In progress

Civic-Generative Liberty: From Formal Rights to Reproducible Freedom

Develops the idea that liberty is not sustained by rights alone, but by the civic and institutional capacities through which people repeatedly exercise, defend, interpret, and extend those rights. This project connects constitutional order, local self-government, associational life, and the practical grammar of freedom.

Research program

Capital, Time, and the Structure of Production

Develops a time-structured account of production, savings, fund flows, monetary spending, excess capacity, and adjustment across stages of production. This work connects Austrian capital theory, monetary theory, and macroeconomic coordination through a temporal theory of production.

Research notes

Civic work

Bihar, law, and practical liberty

A parallel public-facing strand of my work focuses on Bihar and India: how legal reform, entrepreneurship, local self-government, civic education, and institutional imagination can help ordinary people move from dependence on patronage toward the practical exercise of liberty.

This is not separate from the research program. It is the applied version of the same theoretical question: how do people acquire the grammar, confidence, forums, and institutional habits required to make rules work in actual social life?

The broader aim is civic-generative liberty: a form of freedom that is not merely declared from above, but generated from below through repeated claim-making, association, enterprise, legal competence, local responsibility, and resistance to arbitrary power.
