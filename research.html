Research

Making rules work.

My research asks how institutional orders become intelligible, credible, and liberty-generating. Formal rules do not operate by themselves. They must be understood as rules, routed through credible forums, stabilized through associational mechanisms, and reproduced through civic habits capable of sustaining impersonal constraint over time.

Dissertation arc

Making Rules Work: Essays on Civic Grammar, Credible Coordination, and Constitutional Capacity in New Institutional Economics

01

Institutional Intelligibility

The first problem is meaning. Rules can constrain action only after actors possess the categories through which conduct becomes institutionally legible. A petition must be recognized as a petition, a court filing as a claim, an office as an office, a constitutional violation as a public wrong, and a legal judgment as an authoritative settlement rather than a temporary victory.

02

Credible Coordination

The second problem is operational credibility. Even when rules are intelligible, they must remain usable under dispersed knowledge, strategic behavior, factual uncertainty, and disagreement. Credible coordination capacity names the order-level ability to keep signals intelligible, rivalry contestable, disputes routable, defections sanctionable, and errors repairable while coordination remains underway.

03

Civic-Generative Capacity

The third problem is constitutional reproduction. Durable liberty requires more than formal rights or written constraints. It depends on a historically accumulated population-level capacity to sustain impersonal rules through effective habits, effective beliefs, local self-government, associational life, and repeated civic apprenticeship.

Research architecture

From rules as constraints to rules as lived institutional capacities

The program extends New Institutional Economics from within. It accepts the importance of rules, incentives, credible commitment, transaction costs, and equilibrium, but argues that these concepts presuppose deeper conditions: interpretive competence, associational coordination, and civic reproduction.

1. Civic Grammar: the syntax of public action

Civic grammar is the historically evolved structure of meanings, interpretive habits, procedural expectations, and public competences through which people learn to act institutionally. It is not culture in general, not mere trust, and not simply a focal point. It is the grammar through which injuries become claims, claims become grievances, grievances enter procedures, procedures invoke authority, and authority becomes distinguishable from arbitrary power.

Meaning

2. Credible Coordination Capacity: the ecology of rule-working

Credible coordination capacity explains how institutional orders remain action-guiding in live settings. It identifies five outputs that any coordinating order must provide: rule articulation, forum routing, fact production, sanctioning, and repair. The focus is not merely whether actors are bound ex ante, but whether rules remain intelligible, contestable, and revisable while decentralized interaction continues.

Coordination

3. Civic-Generative Capacity: the social infrastructure of constitutional liberty

Civic-generative capacity explains why similarly designed constitutions can produce radically different trajectories. Constitutional rules do not mechanically generate the civic dispositions they require. They stabilize and scale capacities that must already be partly present: habits of mediated coordination, beliefs in impersonal restraint, local competence, and the willingness to defend general rules even when short-run political advantage favors discretion.

Current manuscripts

Core papers

These papers form the central research sequence. Each develops one layer of the larger problem: how rules become meaningful, how coordination becomes credible, and how liberty becomes reproducible across time.

Institutions as Fields of Meaning: Civic Grammar and the Interpretive Turn in Economics

This paper argues that institutional economics has treated a crucial condition as given: that actors already know how to interpret institutions. It develops the concept of civic grammar to explain how rules become publicly intelligible before they can become regulative. The paper distinguishes the constitutive function of institutions from their regulative function, showing why courts, contracts, parliaments, property rights, offices, and constitutional procedures cannot work as mere incentive devices unless actors first understand what kinds of acts, claims, violations, and authorities they are encountering.

The historical case traces the English Parliament’s evolution from private petitioning toward common grievance, procedural contestation, and institutionalized public claim-making. The larger theoretical move is to reinterpret credible commitment as dependent upon a prior field of institutional intelligibility.

Working paper

Endogenous Legitimacy in Market Orders: Civil Society as the Associational Ground of Credible Coordination Capacity

This paper reconstructs legitimacy in decentralized market orders as credible coordination capacity. Rather than treating legitimacy as something supplied from outside by state regulation or corporate purpose, it treats legitimacy as an endogenous property of institutional ecologies that can keep coordination meaningful under incomplete knowledge, error, and contestation.

The paper identifies five outputs required for CCC: rule articulation, forum routing, fact production, sanctioning, and repair. It then distinguishes those outputs from the specific associational mechanisms that generate them, allowing institutional analysis to see how orders lose mechanisms, reconstitute substitutes, and preserve coordinative capacity through shifted means. The diamond bourse ecology serves as the main comparative case.

In progress

Civic-Generative Capacity and Durability of Constitutional Orders

This paper argues that constitutional durability cannot be explained by institutional design alone. Against theories that treat constitutions as equilibrium-generating devices, it advances the concept of civic-generative capacity: the historically accumulated, population-level capacity to sustain impersonal constitutional restraint through decentralized civic practice.

The paper develops the relationship between effective habits and effective beliefs. Effective habits are recurrent repertoires of mediated conduct through which actors process disputes, grievances, and coordination problems without immediately resorting to discretionary command. Effective beliefs make rule-governed conduct publicly intelligible, legitimate, and normatively assessable. The comparative argument contrasts Anglo-American civic apprenticeship with the administrative centralization of ancien régime France.

Broader research lines

Related projects

Beyond the core dissertation sequence, my work extends into capital theory, legal reform, constitutional political economy, and the institutional foundations of practical liberty.

Capital, Time, and the Structure of Production

A time-structured account of production, savings, fund flows, monetary spending, excess capacity, and adjustment across stages of production. This line of work connects Austrian capital theory, monetary theory, and macroeconomic coordination by treating production as a temporally organized structure rather than an instantaneous aggregate.

Law, Polycentric Order, and Civic Legal Pluralism

A legal-institutional research line on how legal orders can be rebuilt around property, contract, tort, jury trial, private ordering, voluntary adjudication, and civic courts. The central concern is how law can become less bureaucratic and more rooted in associational competence, procedural accountability, and decentralized dispute resolution.

Research agenda

Bihar, Self-Government, and the Political Economy of Practical Liberty

A public-facing research and institution-building agenda focused on Bihar and India: legal reform, entrepreneurship, local self-government, civic education, and the reconstruction of everyday institutional competence. This work asks how a society trapped in patronage, bureaucratic dependence, and political mediation can develop the civic and economic capacities required for bottom-up liberty.

Applied work

Unifying question

How does liberty become socially reproducible?

The common thread across these projects is the movement from formal design to lived capacity. A constitution may declare limits, a court may possess jurisdiction, a contract may define obligations, and a market may generate prices. But none of these institutions can function as intended unless people possess the learned competences required to understand, use, contest, repair, and reproduce them.

This is why the research program moves across three levels. Civic grammar explains how institutional action becomes meaningful. Credible coordination capacity explains how rules remain operational in decentralized settings. Civic-generative capacity explains how those capacities accumulate into the civic foundation of constitutional liberty.

The deeper aim is to understand liberty not as a merely declared ideal, but as a durable institutional achievement: something generated through practices, associations, habits, beliefs, procedures, and repeated public action over time.
